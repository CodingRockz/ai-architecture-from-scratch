## Overview

AI doesn't just improve existing companies — it changes what a company *can be*. **AI-native**
organisations are built around AI from the start: leaner, faster, with products and operations that
assume AI as a core capability. This lesson is about that strategic shift and the "post-SaaS"
thinking it's provoking.

## Why this matters

For founders and strategists, this is the big-picture opportunity (and threat): the basis of
competition is changing. Companies that organise around AI can do more with fewer people and build
products that weren't possible before — while incumbents organised around old assumptions may be
disrupted. Thinking at this level is part of being an AI architect for a business, not just a system.

## Core concepts

- **AI-native vs AI-added.** AI-added bolts AI onto existing structures; AI-native designs the
  organisation, products, and processes around AI from the ground up (recall "redesign, don't pave
  the cow path," Track 3).
- **Leaner organisations.** Small teams achieving what once needed large ones, by directing AI for
  much of the execution (the whole premise of this course, at company scale).
- **AI-native products.** Products whose core value *is* AI capability, often personalised and
  adaptive in ways traditional software isn't.
- **Post-SaaS thinking.** As AI can build and adapt software cheaply and deliver outcomes directly,
  some question the classic SaaS model (fixed tools sold per seat) — shifting toward outcome- and
  agent-based offerings. Early and speculative, but directionally important.

## How to think about it

```architect
The architect-as-business-strategist asks "if we designed this company/product around AI from
scratch, what would change?" — not "where can we add AI to what we have?" They look for AI-native
advantages (leaner execution, adaptive products, new offerings) while applying the hype lens to
"post-SaaS" claims. The mindset is opportunity-and-disruption: AI changes the basis of competition,
so revisit assumptions you took as fixed.
```

## Decision framework

```decision
title: Where could AI-native thinking change my strategy?
Could a process/product be redesigned around AI rather than augmented? → Look for the bigger, native opportunity.
Could a small AI-directed team achieve what used to need a big one? → A leaner-org advantage.
Is your product's value something AI could now deliver more directly/adaptively? → An AI-native product angle.
Are incumbents in your space vulnerable to an AI-native competitor? → Threat and/or opportunity.
Is a "post-SaaS" claim concrete or hype? → Apply skepticism; act on demonstrated shifts, not slogans.
```

## Common mistakes

- **AI-added thinking only** — bolting AI on while competitors rethink from scratch.
- **Over-believing "post-SaaS" hype** — restructuring on speculation rather than demonstrated
  change.
- **Ignoring the disruption risk** — assuming your model is safe from AI-native challengers.
- **Forgetting governance scales too** — a leaner AI-heavy org still needs the controls from Track
  5 (arguably more, with fewer human checks).

## Real business examples

- A small startup competes with much larger incumbents by running AI-directed operations across
  support, marketing, and ops — doing with a handful of people what used to need dozens.
- A product is reimagined as an adaptive AI-native experience rather than a fixed feature set,
  changing its value proposition.

## Governance considerations

```governance
AI-native and leaner organisations concentrate more activity into AI systems with fewer human checkpoints — which raises, not lowers, the importance of governance. Fewer people means fewer informal catches, so the formal controls (oversight on high-stakes actions, evaluation, auditability, access control, cost limits) matter more. As you redesign around AI, design the governance in at the same time (Track 5) — an AI-native company that skips controls is an incident-native company.
```

## Key takeaways

- AI-native companies are **built around AI from the start** (vs bolting it on) — **leaner orgs,
  adaptive products, new offerings**.
- It changes the **basis of competition** — opportunity for the nimble, disruption risk for
  incumbents.
- **"Post-SaaS" is directional but hyped** — act on demonstrated shifts, not slogans.
- Leaner AI-heavy orgs need **more governance**, not less — design controls in as you redesign.
