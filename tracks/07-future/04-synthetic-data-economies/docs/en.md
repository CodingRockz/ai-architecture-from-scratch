## Overview

As high-quality human data gets scarcer and privacy rules tighten, **synthetic data** — data
generated by AI to train or improve other AI — is becoming central. It can fill gaps, protect
privacy, and create training examples cheaply. But it carries real pitfalls. This lesson is how to
think about the synthetic-data trend.

## Why this matters

Synthetic data affects model quality (where the next improvements come from), privacy (it can
reduce reliance on real personal data), and your own projects (it can help when you lack training
data — relevant to fine-tuning). It's also a place where naive use causes quiet failures.

## Core concepts

- **What it is.** AI-generated examples used to train or fine-tune models, or to augment scarce/
  sensitive real data.
- **The upsides:** fills data gaps, covers rare/edge cases on demand, and can reduce use of real
  personal data (a privacy benefit).
- **The pitfalls:**
  - **Model collapse / quality decay.** Training repeatedly on AI-generated data can degrade quality
    — the model amplifies its own biases and loses the richness of real data.
  - **Bias amplification.** Synthetic data inherits and can magnify the generator's biases.
  - **False confidence.** It can look diverse while missing real-world messiness.
- **Best practice:** synthetic data usually works best **mixed with real data** and **validated**,
  not as a wholesale replacement.

## How to think about it

```architect
The architect treats synthetic data as a useful supplement, not a free substitute for real data. For their own fine-tuning, they'll use it to cover gaps and edge cases — then validate the result on *real* tasks (Track 6), because synthetic data can quietly mislead. They watch the broader trend (where model improvements come from) with the hype lens, and they value synthetic data's privacy upside where it reduces reliance on real personal data.
```

## Decision framework

```decision
title: Should I use synthetic data?
Lacking training data or specific edge cases? → Synthetic data can fill gaps — then validate on real cases.
Privacy-sensitive domain? → Synthetic data may reduce reliance on real personal data (a benefit) — but verify it doesn't leak patterns from real data it was based on.
Tempted to train mostly on synthetic data? → Be cautious — quality can decay; mix with real data.
Using it for fine-tuning? → Validate the resulting model on REAL tasks, not synthetic ones.
```

## Common mistakes

- **Training mostly/only on synthetic data** — risking quality decay and bias amplification.
- **Not validating on real data** — synthetic looks fine but the model fails on real inputs.
- **Assuming synthetic = automatically private** — poorly generated synthetic data can still leak
  patterns from the real data behind it.
- **Ignoring inherited bias** in the generator.

## Real business examples

- A team lacking examples of a rare scenario generates synthetic ones to augment a small real
  dataset for fine-tuning — and validates the model on real cases before trusting it.
- A privacy-sensitive project uses synthetic data to reduce handling of real personal records,
  carefully checking the synthetic set doesn't reproduce identifiable real data.

## Governance considerations

```governance
Synthetic data has a privacy upside (less reliance on real personal data) but isn't automatically safe: data generated from real records can sometimes leak identifiable patterns, so validate privacy, not just assume it. It can also amplify bias from the generator — a fairness concern for decisions about people. And quality decay from over-reliance is a reliability risk. Govern synthetic data like any training data: check rights/provenance, test for bias and privacy leakage, and validate the resulting model on real tasks.
```

## Key takeaways

- **Synthetic data** (AI-generated training data) fills gaps, covers edge cases, and can **reduce
  use of real personal data**.
- Pitfalls: **quality decay (model collapse), bias amplification, false confidence** — usually best
  **mixed with real data**.
- For your fine-tuning: use it to fill gaps, but **validate the model on real tasks**.
- It's **not automatically private** or unbiased — govern and verify it like any training data.
